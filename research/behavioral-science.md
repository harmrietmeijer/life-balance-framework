# The Behavioral Science Behind the Life Balance Framework

This document outlines the evidence-based principles that inform the framework.

## 1. PERMA Model (Seligman, 2011)

Martin Seligman's model of flourishing identifies five elements of wellbeing: Positive Emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Achievement. The Life Balance Framework maps these across our three pillars:

- **Happy** → Positive Emotions, Relationships, Meaning
- **Healthy** → Engagement (flow states), physical foundation for all elements
- **Wealthy** → Achievement, Meaning (through purposeful work)

## 2. COM-B Model (Michie et al., 2011)

The COM-B framework explains that behavior change requires three components:

- **Capability** — Do you have the knowledge and skills?
- **Opportunity** — Does your environment support the change?
- **Motivation** — Do you have sufficient drive?

The weekly check-in addresses all three: awareness builds capability, micro-actions create opportunity, and tracking progress fuels motivation through the endowed progress effect.

## 3. Tiny Habits (Fogg, 2019)

BJ Fogg's research demonstrates that the most effective way to build new habits is:

1. Make the behavior tiny (2 minutes or less)
2. Attach it to an existing routine (habit stacking)
3. Celebrate immediately after

The framework's micro-actions follow this model — small enough to start today, attached to routines you already have.

## 4. MINDSPACE Framework (Dolan et al., 2012)

The MINDSPACE mnemonic captures nine of the most robust behavioral influences:

- **M**essenger — We're influenced by who communicates information
- **I**ncentives — Our responses are shaped by mental shortcuts
- **N**orms — We're influenced by what others do
- **D**efaults — We tend to go with the default option
- **S**alience — Our attention is drawn to what seems relevant
- **P**riming — Our behavior is influenced by subconscious cues
- **A**ffect — Emotional responses shape our actions
- **C**ommitments — We seek to be consistent with promises
- **E**go — We act in ways that make us feel good about ourselves

The Life Balance Score leverages salience (making imbalance visible), commitments (weekly check-in ritual), and ego (tracking improvement feels rewarding).

## 5. Endowed Progress Effect (Nunes & Drèze, 2006)

People are more motivated to complete a goal when they feel they've already made progress. By scoring your current state, you start from a non-zero position — you're not starting from scratch, you're improving from where you are.
